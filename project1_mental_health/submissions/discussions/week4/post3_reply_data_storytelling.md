[Reply to classmate's post about presenting data findings]

Your point about visualization being undervalued in technical projects is something I have felt throughout this program. It is easy to spend most of the project time on modeling and treat the charts as an afterthought, but in practice the chart is often the only thing a decision-maker sees.

What I have learned is that the design choices in a visualization are themselves analytical decisions. Choosing a KDE over a histogram is not just aesthetic — it signals something about how you want the reader to interpret distribution shape. Choosing to annotate a threshold line on an ROC curve tells the audience where the trade-off actually lives, rather than letting them guess. These are judgment calls that require understanding both the data and the audience.

There is also a real skill in knowing when not to show a chart. Sometimes a single well-chosen statistic communicates more clearly than a complex figure. The goal is clarity, not comprehensiveness. The rubric in DSC680 explicitly rewards storytelling through visualization, and I think that is a genuinely useful habit to build for professional work — the best analysis in the world does not help anyone if it cannot be understood.
