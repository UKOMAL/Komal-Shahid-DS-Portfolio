Staying in the same domain makes a lot of sense strategically — you accumulate context that actually matters for the modeling decisions, and you do not burn the first week just understanding what the data represents. I think there is a real cost to domain-switching that does not show up in the rubric: you end up making more superficial feature engineering choices because you are still learning what the variables mean.

That said, I am deliberately switching domains for exactly the reason you mentioned — deliberately choosing a hard contrast. Mental health survey data is relatively clean, balanced, and linear. Financial fraud is messy, imbalanced, and adversarial (fraud patterns drift because fraudsters adapt). Working both problems back to back should force me to generalize my thinking about what parts of the pipeline are universal and what parts are domain-specific. Curious whether your within-domain approach reveals that too — whether the same techniques just transfer or whether you find surprising differences.
