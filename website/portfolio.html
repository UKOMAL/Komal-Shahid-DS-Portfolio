Predicting Treatment-Seeking Behavior in Tech Workers

Applied ML on the OSMI 2016 Mental Health in Tech Survey — from raw survey data to an interpretable, fair, and actionable classifier.

The Problem

Mental health conditions affect approximately 1 in 4 people, yet treatment remains significantly under-utilized in the tech industry due to stigma, access barriers, and organizational gaps. Understanding what drives individuals to seek treatment is critical for HR teams and mental health advocates.

Key research question: What organizational and personal factors most strongly predict whether a tech worker will seek mental health treatment?

Top 5 Predictive Factors

1
Work InterferenceStrongest signal — employees whose conditions interfere with work are far more likely to seek treatment.
2
Family HistoryGenetic predisposition and family norms around mental health correlate with treatment-seeking.
3
Mental Health BenefitsWhen employers provide benefits, treatment rates increase measurably.
4
Previous DiagnosisPrior mental health diagnosis strongly predicts current treatment-seeking.
5
Supervisor SupportPerception of a supportive direct supervisor increases help-seeking behavior.

Model Comparison

Why Logistic Regression won: Marginal performance differences across all four models don't justify the loss of explainability. For HR stakeholders who need to understand why the model flags certain factors, direct coefficient interpretation and SHAP waterfall plots are essential — not optional. A model that isn't interpretable, private, and fair isn't finished.

The Ethical Decision: Feature Exclusion

The novel Employer Support Index (ESI) — a composite feature engineering combining benefits availability, supervisor support, and organizational wellness signals — emerged as the single most predictive feature (highest SHAP impact). However, including employer-specific scores in a deployed model creates a perverse incentive: employers could game their ESI rather than improving actual support.

Decision: The ESI was retained for research insight but flagged for careful consideration before any deployment. The project documents this as a deliberate ethical choice — surfacing the tension between predictive power and responsible use.

The Challenge

Healthcare AI requires large, diverse training datasets — but patient data is the most sensitive data that exists. Hospitals cannot legally or ethically pool raw records. The result: isolated models, siloed training, and AI that doesn't generalize across populations.

Research question: Can diagnostic AI achieve clinically meaningful performance when hospitals train together without sharing a single patient record?

Key Design Decisions

🔒
Differential Privacy (DP-SGD)Gaussian noise injected into gradients before upload. Each site's contribution is mathematically bounded — no individual patient signal survives aggregation.
🏥
FedAvg AggregationWeighted averaging of model updates by local dataset size — larger hospitals contribute proportionally more, improving calibration across population distributions.
⛓️
Blockchain Audit TrailEach aggregation round is hash-verified and logged on an immutable ledger — enabling full regulatory audit of model lineage without exposing training data.
🧠
CNN ArchitectureLightweight convolutional model chosen for medical imaging tasks — balancing representational capacity with communication efficiency across the federation.
The core insight: Federated learning inverts the traditional data pipeline. Instead of moving data to the model, we move the model to the data. Differential privacy ensures that even if gradient updates were intercepted, individual patient records cannot be reconstructed — a formal mathematical guarantee, not just a policy promise.

Impact & Implications

  • Proves clinical-grade accuracy (89% F1) is achievable without data centralization
  • HIPAA-compliant architecture viable for real multi-institution deployment
  • Template for federated AI in any high-stakes privacy domain: finance, law, education
  • Blockchain verification enables regulatory audit without revealing training data
  • Dockerized pipeline reduces deployment friction across heterogeneous hospital infrastructure

The Concept

Corner LED billboards in Tokyo and Seoul create stunning 3D illusions where products appear to pop out of the screen. These anamorphic effects rely on precise geometric warping calibrated to the viewer's angle. Manual production costs $5,000–$15,000 per asset. This project automates the geometric warp — both in Python for batch production and in-browser for live previews.

How It Works

1
Depth EstimationSynthetic depth from Laplacian edge detection + Gaussian blur. Optional MiDaS/DPT neural depth for production quality.
2
Radial DisplacementDepth-driven radial warp from center outward — foreground objects push toward the viewer, creating the 3D pop effect.
3
Decorative Elements30+ emoji decoratives placed with computed depth layers — flowers, gems, fire, stars orbit the product at varying z-depths.
4
Corner Billboard RenderOutput split into front and side canvases, simulating the perpendicular LED panels of a real corner billboard.
5
LED Color BoostOptional saturation and brightness enhancement mimicking the vivid output of real LED billboard panels.
6
Angle SimulatorInteractive viewing-angle slider shows how the illusion distorts off-axis — same physics as real installations.

Interactive Features

  • 4 built-in product scenes: Juice bottle, sneaker, smartphone, perfume — each hand-drawn with 3D shading and depth layers
  • Custom image upload: Drag any image into the generator and the pipeline auto-estimates depth from edges
  • Warp strength control: Real-time slider adjusting displacement intensity from subtle to extreme
  • Depth map preview: Toggle to visualize the computed depth field (brighter = closer to viewer)
  • Decorative element picker: Click to toggle 30+ decorative emojis on/off, adjust count and scale
  • Corner billboard visualization: See the output rendered on a perspective-correct 3D billboard model

Ensemble Fraud Detection System

Financial fraud detection on a highly imbalanced real-world transaction dataset (fraud rate: 0.27%). A three-model ensemble — PyTorch Autoencoder (reconstruction-error scoring), Isolation Forest, and XGBoost — with SHAP feature attribution for explainability. Primary metric: AUPRC to handle extreme class imbalance.

97.8%

Ensemble models
Key contributions
  • Autoencoder trained on normal transactions; anomalies flagged by high reconstruction error
  • Isolation Forest for unsupervised outlier detection as a complementary signal
  • XGBoost trained on labeled fraud/non-fraud with SMOTE for class rebalancing
  • Ensemble score fusion via weighted probability averaging
  • SHAP waterfall plots for per-prediction explanation — critical for compliance
  • AUPRC used as primary metric (F1 and ROC alone mislead on 0.27% imbalance)
Why it matters

Financial fraud detection requires two things simultaneously: high recall (catch fraud) and high precision (avoid false accusations). Ensemble methods outperform any single model here because the autoencoder catches novel unseen patterns while XGBoost captures known fraud signatures — and SHAP makes the decisions auditable for regulators.
