Post 4: Ethics — carrying good habits through the rest of the program
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Hi everyone —

With DSC680 and the rest of the MSDS work ahead, I have been thinking about ethics less as a one-time “compliance paragraph” in a proposal and more as a set of daily habits. My Project 1 uses mental health and workplace survey data, which is a good reminder of how quickly data science can touch sensitive topics. For me, staying ethical through the end of the program means a few concrete commitments: keep reporting at the group level for anything that could affect real people, document sampling bias and limitations in plain language, and refuse storylines that sound more certain than the evidence supports. I also want to keep checking whether my visualizations and takeaways could be misread as individual diagnosis or HR screening, because that is not the intent of my project and should never be implied.

Academic integrity is part of the same picture. I plan to attribute code I did not write, cite sources when I paraphrase or quote, and use collaboration the way the program intends—to learn—not to recycle someone else’s narrative or results. If I use generative tools for brainstorming or editing, I still need to verify claims, keep the analysis mine, and follow whatever the course policy says in Canvas. The APA’s recent workplace well-being reporting also reinforces that how we talk about mental health at work matters for stigma and trust (American Psychological Association, 2023), which affects how I will word recommendations in the white paper.

For anyone else in capstone mode: what ethical guardrails are you building in now so you are not scrambling to retrofit them at Milestone 3?

Reference
American Psychological Association. (2023). 2023 Work in America Survey: Workplaces as engines of psychological health and well-being. https://www.apa.org/pubs/reports/work-in-america/2023-workplace-health-well-being

Post 5: Peer review swap — project plan, methods, and how I will present results
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

I would really appreciate some peer feedback as I move from the Milestone 1 proposal into the draft white paper. My Project 1 is predicting mental health treatment-seeking among tech workers using the OSMI survey, with Logistic Regression, Random Forest, XGBoost, and SVM, plus SMOTE on training folds only and SHAP for interpretation. I am relying on SHAP because it gives a consistent way to explain feature contributions across model types (Lundberg & Lee, 2017), but translating that into a white paper is a different challenge. If anyone is willing to skim a short outline or later a draft figure deck, I am especially unsure about two presentation choices: whether to lead the results section with one “best” model or show a small model comparison table first, and whether SHAP beeswarm plots will be enough for a non-technical reader or if I should pair every figure with a short “so what” caption.

I also want a sanity check on my plan of attack after the merge of multi-year survey files—does it seem reasonable to prioritize a clean feature dictionary and clear missing-value rules before tuning, or would you spend more time up front on EDA visuals for the story? Feedback on tone matters too; I am trying to balance technical credibility with accessibility for stakeholders who are not data scientists.

I am happy to return the favor with portfolio or project reviews this term. If you have favorite books, newsletters, or online communities that helped you learn how to explain models to mixed audiences, please drop them—I am always looking for good examples of clear technical writing.

Thanks in advance.

Reference
Lundberg, S. M., & Lee, S.-I. (2017). A unified approach to interpreting model predictions. In Advances in Neural Information Processing Systems 30 (pp. 4765–4774). Curran Associates.
