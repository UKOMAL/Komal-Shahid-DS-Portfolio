Who Seeks Help? Predicting Mental Health Treatment in the Tech Workforce

An end-to-end data science investigation into the workplace factors that determine whether a tech employee seeks professional mental health treatment — and what companies can do about it.

OSMI Survey 2016 · N=1,259 4 Classifiers · 5-Fold CV SHAP Interpretability Fairness Audit APA-Cited Sources
01 · Business Problem

A $1 Trillion Problem Hidden in Plain Sight

WHO estimates depression and anxiety cost the global economy ~$1 trillion/year in lost productivity (WHO, 2022). In tech, stigma, unclear benefits, and confidentiality fears keep employees from seeking care even when coverage exists.

Research goal: Use 22 workplace and demographic features from the OSMI survey to train classifiers that predict treatment-seeking, then use SHAP to identify the employer levers with the highest potential impact — giving HR teams an evidence-based roadmap, not a screening tool.

02 · Data

The OSMI Survey: A Rare Lens on Tech Mental Health

Open Sourcing Mental Illness (osmihelp.org) has collected voluntary, anonymised responses from tech workers since 2014. The 2016 wave is the benchmark for this analysis.

Raw Import

1,259 rows × 27 columns. Target: treatment (binary). Near-balanced classes (49.7% / 50.3%).

Age Filtering

Removed 21 implausible ages (< 18 or > 75). Final N = 1,259 clean records.

Gender Standardisation

50+ free-text entries mapped to 3 canonical labels: Male · Female · Non-binary/Other. Preserves subgroup signal for the fairness audit.

Feature Engineering

Employer Support Index (ESI): A composite 0–4 score summing four binary employer indicators — benefits, care options, wellness program, and anonymity protection.

Encoding & Imputation

Ordinal Likert fields (work_interfere, leave) encoded numerically. Missing work_interfere (~22%) imputed as modal value "Rarely."

Key data fact: The OSMI dataset is publicly available at Kaggle (OSMI, 2016) and has no PII. All results are reported at group level, consistent with responsible secondary data use.

03 · Exploratory Analysis

What the Data Reveals Before Any Model

Chi-square tests and visual EDA surface the structural patterns that drive treatment-seeking — and distinguish signal from noise.

Difference in treatment-seeking rate between employees who report mental health "Often" vs "Never" interfering with work. The most powerful individual-level signal in the dataset.

Fairness takeaway: Neither gender nor age was statistically significant in chi-square testing. This means the demographic gaps observed in Figure 11 are largely explained by differences in employer support received, not by inherent demographic traits. Fixing employer support structure is likely to close demographic gaps at the same time.

06 · Recommendations

Four Actions, Ranked by Evidence

Derived directly from SHAP importances and ESI analysis. Not generic HR advice — model-backed priorities.

01

Complete the ESI Four

Ensure all four ESI components are in place: mental health benefits, care option awareness, wellness program, and anonymity protection. Companies at ESI 0–1 show 15+ point lower treatment rates.

02

Target Early Interference

Work interference is the top SHAP predictor. Train managers on early-stage mental health check-ins — before employees reach the "Often" threshold where the gap widens sharply.

03

Simplify Leave Access

Leave difficulty is a top-5 SHAP feature. Streamlining the mental health leave request process removes a structural barrier that benefit availability alone cannot solve.

04

Track ESI as a KPI

Administer the OSMI survey adaptation annually. Use ESI score delta as a measurable HR KPI connecting investment in mental health infrastructure to treatment rate outcomes.

07 · Ethics

Built-In Guardrails

Mental health data demands responsibility at every step.

🔒

Privacy First

All data is publicly anonymised OSMI survey data. No PII. Results reported at group level only — never individual.

🚫

Not an HR Screener

This model is a policy diagnostic tool. It must never be used to score, flag, or profile individual employees for HR decisions.

🗣️

Person-First Language

All analysis uses person-first framing. "Sought treatment" — not "diagnosed." Reduces stigma reinforcement in reporting.

📏

Scope Transparency

The OSMI sample is not representative of all tech workers globally. English-language, voluntary, US-heavy. Findings explicitly scoped accordingly.

⚖️

Fairness Audit

Demographic subgroup rates reported transparently. Disparities contextualised — not erased or overclaimed.

🔗

Associative, Not Causal

Cross-sectional data supports associations only. No causal language used in the analysis or recommendations.
